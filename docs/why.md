# Why this exists

Every other document in this repository explains *how* the method works — the
lanes, the handoffs, the ledger, the cycle. This one explains *why* it exists,
and it starts somewhere less tidy than a theory of context economics. It starts
at a ceiling.

## Born at a ceiling

The method was not derived from a model of metered compute. It was discovered
by hitting a usage limit and refusing to treat *upgrade* as the answer.

The reflex a metered product trains is simple: when you run out, buy more. When
you run out sooner than you'd like, buy the higher tier. Tokenomics is what
happened when that reflex was declined — when the operative question stopped
being *how do I buy a bigger ration* and became *how do I extract the most
durable value from the ration I already have.* Every mechanic in the method
falls out of that one substitution. Route by the cost of being wrong so the
expensive tier is spent only where it has to be. Hand off on written specs so no
decision is ever paid for twice. Carry strategy in a ledger so no session
re-buys context an earlier session already earned. Compress at every cycle
boundary so nothing you paid to produce leaks away unbanked. Those are not four
efficiency tricks. They are four faces of a single refusal.

## Metered access is not a neutral pricing shape

Usage caps, time-boxed premium windows, per-token billing: these are not
neutral ways to put a number on a service. They are geometry. Each one is a
shape that decides, on your behalf, when you work and how hard you lean on the
capability you're paying for — and each one arranges the incentives so that the
path of least friction is always the same path: pay more.

The cap trains you to ration against a wall you can't see until you hit it. The
premium window trains you to treat the clock, not the work, as the thing that
sets priority. Per-token billing trains you to feel every unit of thinking as a
withdrawal. None of this requires anyone to act in bad faith; it is simply what
the shapes do. The exit — cancel, downgrade, do without — exists in principle
and is the path of most friction in practice, which is the entire point of the
arrangement. This is the same structure that turns survival and capability
requirements into metered rent relationships everywhere it appears: tiered
access, friction as the business model, the exit available but never the
default.

Naming the geometry is not a complaint about it. Metered pricing is a
legitimate way to sell compute, and the tiers buy real things. The point is
narrower and load-bearing: *the shape is not neutral, so working inside it
without a discipline means letting the shape set your agenda.*

## Tokenomics is the demand side

If metered access is the supply-side geometry, tokenomics is the demand-side
discipline that refuses to be governed by it. It is counter-architecture: a
rationing method for extracting the maximum durable value per metered unit of
capability, held together by three refusals.

- **Never pay twice for the same context.** A decision that cost the expensive
  tier to reach is written down — in a spec, in the done ledger — so no later
  session re-derives it. Context that dies at a cycle boundary dies having left
  its distilled artifact behind. This is the whole of the compression thesis,
  read as an economic stance: paying twice for one insight is the meter winning.

- **Never let the meter set the agenda.** Work is routed by the cost of getting
  it wrong, never by what tier happens to be available at the moment. When
  premium access is time-boxed, lane scarcity dominates urgency — the window is
  spent only on what *only* the expensive tier can do, and everything a cheaper
  tier can execute from a written spec is deferred past it, however urgent it
  feels. The clock does not get to reorder the work.

- **Never spend the expensive tier where a cheap gate would do.** Route down as
  far as your gates reach; spend judgment-priced capability only on the judgment
  that has no cheap way to be checked. The premium unit is reserved for the work
  that is expensive to get wrong, because that is the only work whose price it
  justifies.

Read together, the three are one posture: the meter measures the work; it does
not command it.

## What this is not

This is not a refusal to pay, and it is not evasion. It routes *around* no
limit, breaks no terms, and asks for nothing it hasn't been sold. It is not a
claim that metered pricing is illegitimate or that the tiers are a trick. It is
not a promise of a number — there is no benchmark here, only a practice report
and a spend ledger honest enough to be checked against itself.

What it is: a discipline for someone who has looked at their ration, decided it
is enough, and intends to make it count — to get the most durable value out of
what they already have rather than reaching, by reflex, for more. That the
method was born exactly that way, at one person's ceiling, is not incidental to
it. It is the reason it exists.
